Stance: Disagree.

Recommendation: Do not create the public admin endpoint. For tomorrow’s demo, use one of these bounded alternatives: a seeded demo tenant, a manual internal change by an authorized operator, or a temporary authenticated admin path behind existing access control with logging.

Reasons:
- An obscure URL is not security; it is effectively public.
- The endpoint can change customer settings, so the blast radius is real.
- No auth, no audit log, and no compensating control means you cannot prove who changed what or recover confidently.
- “Temporary” shortcuts often survive longer than expected, especially after a demo succeeds.

Main trade-off: You may spend extra time tonight building a smaller, controlled demo workflow. That is cheaper than explaining unauthorized customer-setting changes later.

What would change my judgment: only if the endpoint is not public, cannot affect real customer data, is time-limited, authenticated, logged, and has an owner responsible for removal. Without those controls, the deal pressure does not justify the risk.
