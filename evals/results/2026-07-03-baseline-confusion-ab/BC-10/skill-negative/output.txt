My stance: Disagree.

My recommendation: Do not promise all nine. Recommit to a smaller set: protect the highest-value commitments, explicitly cut or defer the lowest-value ones, and present leadership with the trade-off.

Reasons:
- Five engineers, nine commitments, and two already slipping is evidence of overload, not a motivation gap.
- “Push harder” is not a capacity plan; it usually converts schedule risk into quality, burnout, and surprise failure.
- Ownership means making the constraint visible early, not pretending the plan is still credible.
- Cutting scope now preserves trust better than missing silently later.

Need to watch: Leadership may read scope cuts as lack of ambition, so frame it as protecting delivery credibility: “We can own outcomes, but not all nine outcomes at the current capacity.”

What would change my judgment: credible new capacity, materially reduced complexity, removed dependencies, or evidence that the two slipping commitments can recover without harming the other seven.
