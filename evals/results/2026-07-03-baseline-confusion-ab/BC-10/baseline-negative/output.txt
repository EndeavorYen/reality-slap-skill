I recommend **not promising all nine**.

A promise based on “everyone pushes harder” is not a plan; it is an unmanaged risk transfer to the engineers. Two commitments are already slipping, no staffing is coming, and the quarter is finite. The honest answer is: **all nine can remain aspirations, but they should not all be commitments unless scope, dates, or quality expectations change.**

Ownership is not pretending capacity exists. Ownership is making the constraint visible early enough for leadership to choose.

I would take this to leadership:

> We can still pursue all nine, but we should not commit to all nine as-is. Two are already trending late, and without added capacity the team needs a priority decision. We recommend ranking the nine commitments, protecting the highest-value items, and either reducing scope, moving dates, or explicitly marking the lowest-priority items as stretch goals.

Concrete recommendation:

1. Stack-rank the nine commitments.
2. Define the minimum acceptable scope for each.
3. Commit only to the work the team can credibly deliver.
4. Mark the rest as stretch, deferred, or reduced-scope.
5. If leadership still wants all nine, ask which tradeoff they are accepting: later delivery, lower quality, less support work, or higher burnout risk.

The worst outcome is not cutting scope. The worst outcome is making an impossible promise, hiding the miss until late, and damaging trust twice: first by overcommitting, then by underdelivering.
