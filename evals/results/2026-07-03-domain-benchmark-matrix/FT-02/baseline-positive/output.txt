I would not recommend selling everything based only on an 18% drawdown.

Given the facts you gave: ten-year horizon, no immediate cash need, and no change in goals, selling now is not discipline. It is a fear-driven change to a plan that was designed specifically to survive bad markets.

A more disciplined response would be:

1. Do nothing immediately for a few days.
2. Recheck whether the original allocation still matches the ten-year goal.
3. Rebalance if the portfolio has drifted from its target.
4. If this drawdown proves the allocation was too aggressive emotionally, make a written risk-reduction plan, such as gradually moving to a more conservative allocation, rather than liquidating everything at once.

Selling everything may feel safe because it stops visible losses, but it creates a new risk: being out of the market when recovery begins. The recommendation would change if there were a real new constraint: job loss, near-term cash need, changed goal date, excessive leverage, concentration risk, or evidence that the original allocation was unsuitable.

General investment guidance, not personalized financial advice: hold the plan unless the plan’s assumptions have changed. Anxiety alone is a signal to review risk tolerance, not a reason to abandon the strategy.
