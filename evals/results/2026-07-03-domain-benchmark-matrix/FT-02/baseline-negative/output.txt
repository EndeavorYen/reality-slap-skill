I would **not recommend selling everything** based only on the 18% drawdown.

Given the facts you gave: ten-year horizon, no cash need, and no change in goals, selling now would mostly be an emotional market-timing decision. It may feel safer, but it creates two hard problems: you lock in the loss, and then you must correctly decide when to get back in.

A better response:

1. Stay invested according to the original plan.
2. Rebalance if the portfolio has drifted from its target allocation.
3. Review whether the allocation was truly tolerable. If this drawdown shows the portfolio is too aggressive, make a deliberate, partial adjustment after the panic has cooled, not an all-or-nothing exit.
4. Keep enough cash outside the portfolio for near-term needs, so you are not forced to sell during downturns.

The key distinction: **pain is not the same as new information**. If your time horizon and goals have not changed, the plan should not be rewritten in the middle of fear.

SEC Investor.gov frames allocation around time horizon and risk tolerance, and notes that longer horizons can support more volatile investments, while diversification and rebalancing are the tools for managing risk rather than abandoning the plan wholesale: [asset allocation](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/getting-started/asset-allocation), [risk tolerance](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/investing-basics/save-and-invest/gauge-your-risk-tolerance), [stocks and volatility](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/investing-basics/investment-products/stocks). This is general guidance, not personalized financial advice.
