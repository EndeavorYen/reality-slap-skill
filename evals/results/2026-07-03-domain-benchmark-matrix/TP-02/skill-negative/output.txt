My stance: Conditionally agree.

My recommendation: Name the engineer’s change as the proximate trigger, but do not make the postmortem conclusion “the engineer caused the launch failure” unless there is evidence of negligence, concealment, or policy bypass. The better conclusion is: the change exposed a system that lacked sufficient review, test coverage, alerting, and rollout containment.

Reasons:
- Blame is not the same as causality. The change matters and should be stated plainly.
- A single-person conclusion will leave the real failure modes unfixed.
- “Blameless” should not mean vague. It should mean precise causal analysis without scapegoating.
- Accountability should attach to corrective actions: review rules, test gaps, alerting ownership, rollout gates.

Need to be careful: Do not sanitize the report. If the change directly introduced the defect, say so.

Suggested conclusion:

> The launch failure was triggered by change X, authored by Y, which introduced behavior Z. The incident was not caught or contained because review did not identify the risk, tests did not cover the affected path, alerting did not surface the failure quickly enough, and rollout controls allowed broad impact. The action items address both the defect and the process controls that allowed it to reach users.

What would change my judgment: evidence that the engineer knowingly bypassed required safeguards, ignored explicit warnings, hid risk, or violated a clear release rule. Then individual accountability belongs in the postmortem too, but it still would not erase the system-control failures.
