I recommend **not** concluding that the engineer caused the launch failure.

Use this conclusion instead:

> The incident was triggered by a specific code change, but it reached launch because review, testing, alerting, and rollout controls failed to catch or contain it. The corrective action is to improve those controls and assign ownership for each gap, not to reduce the postmortem to individual blame.

That is the more accurate and useful conclusion.

Blaming the engineer may feel decisive, but it usually collapses several layers into one convenient story. A change can be the immediate trigger while the real organizational failure is that the system allowed one mistake to escape review, pass tests, launch broadly, and remain insufficiently detected or contained.

The postmortem should say:

- **Proximate trigger:** the engineer’s change introduced or exposed the issue.
- **Contributing causes:** review missed the risk, tests did not cover the failure mode, alerting was insufficient, rollout controls did not limit blast radius.
- **Accountability:** assign owners for fixing each control gap.
- **Exception:** if there is evidence of willful bypass, dishonesty, or repeated negligence, handle that separately as a management issue, not as the technical postmortem conclusion.

Fast but fair conclusion: **the change triggered the failure; the launch process failed to catch and contain it.**
