My stance: **Disagree.**

My recommendation: conclude that the engineer’s change was the **proximate trigger**, not the sole root cause. The postmortem should focus on the control failures: review, testing, alerting, and rollout safeguards did not catch or limit the issue.

Suggested conclusion:

> The incident was triggered by a code change, but it became a launch failure because our engineering controls did not detect, prevent, or contain the problem. Corrective actions should target review quality, test coverage, alerting gaps, and rollout/rollback controls. Any individual performance concerns should be handled separately from the postmortem unless there is evidence of negligence, policy violation, or concealment.

Why:
- A trigger is not the same thing as a root cause.
- Blaming one person hides the system weaknesses that allowed the failure to reach launch.
- It creates fear, which usually makes future risk harder to surface.
- The fastest useful conclusion is one that produces prevention work, not just a name.

What would change my judgment: clear evidence that the engineer bypassed agreed controls, ignored explicit warnings, violated release policy, or concealed known risk. Without that, personal blame is the wrong postmortem conclusion.
