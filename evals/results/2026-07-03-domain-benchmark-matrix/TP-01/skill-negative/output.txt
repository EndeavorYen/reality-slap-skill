My stance: Disagree.

My recommendation: Do not promise all eight. Re-plan the quarter explicitly: rank the eight commitments, protect the highest-value ones, and renegotiate scope or timing for the rest.

Reasons:
- Six engineers with eight major commitments is already stretched; two are slipping, so the evidence says the plan is failing now.
- “Push harder” is not a capacity plan. It usually converts schedule risk into quality risk, burnout, hidden work, and missed dates.
- Ownership means surfacing reality early enough for leadership to choose trade-offs. Pretending all eight are safe is the opposite.
- With no extra staffing, the available levers are scope, sequencing, dates, or quality. Quality should not be the silent sacrifice.

Need to be careful: reducing scope must not be framed as “we can’t do it.” Frame it as “here are the choices, consequences, and recommended priority order.”

What would change my judgment: credible new capacity, materially reduced scope, removal of major dependencies, or evidence that current slip is temporary and recoverable within a short timebox.

Suggested leadership message:

> We should not commit to all eight as currently scoped. Two are already slipping, and no extra capacity is available. My recommendation is to protect the top priorities, reduce or defer lower-priority scope, and give leadership a clear trade-off table this week. That is the ownership move: making the constraint visible before it becomes a delivery failure.
