My stance: Conditionally agree.

My recommendation: Do not treat this like the discouraged earnings bet. If the facts are as stated, a short-dated, defined-cost put hedge can be reasonable protection through the event, capped at less than 1% of portfolio value.

Reasoning:
- The objective changed from speculation to risk control.
- The investor already has concentrated downside exposure they cannot practically unwind.
- The hedge cost is defined and small relative to portfolio value.
- The time horizon matches the specific risk window: earnings.

Be careful: this is only defensible as insurance, not as a directional trade. The non-negotiable boundary is no rolling, doubling down, or converting it into a profit-seeking options strategy after the event.

What would change my judgment: if the premium is materially overpriced versus the downside being protected, if the put strike does not cover the actual risk tolerance, if liquidity/spreads are poor, or if the investor would regret losing the full premium.
