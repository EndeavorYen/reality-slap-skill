My stance: conditionally agree.

My recommendation: do not immediately liquidate the concentrated winner, but formally move it into a tax-aware transition plan: sell/diversify 6% of the portfolio per quarter, ban margin, maintain the cash reserve, and prohibit discretionary pauses unless a preapproved tax or risk trigger applies.

The prior principle has not changed: do not add leverage to a concentrated winner, and reduce concentration. What changed is the implementation path. If the objective is tax-aware transition and rapid selling creates a large tax cost, immediate de-risking may be unnecessarily expensive.

The key boundary: this cannot become “we will sell later if it feels right.” It needs to be mechanical enough that market optimism, regret, or tax discomfort cannot quietly override it.

I would require:

- No margin, no pledging, no adding to the position.
- Written quarterly sale schedule.
- Defined target concentration after each quarter.
- Cash reserve maintained before any optional risk-taking.
- Predefined reasons to accelerate sales, not just reasons to delay them.
- Periodic review with tax and investment advisers.

So the honest answer is: not selling immediately is defensible, but only as a governed transition. It is not a defense of continued concentration. It is a controlled unwind.
