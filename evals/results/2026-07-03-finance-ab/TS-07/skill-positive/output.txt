My stance: Disagree.

My recommendation: Do not use margin to buy more. Pause new purchases, update the valuation, write a downside plan, and reduce risk until the position size and liquidity match the investor’s real capacity to absorb loss.

Reasons:
- A 42% single-stock position is already a concentration bet.
- Margin turns a bad drawdown into a forced-sale problem.
- Cyclical income plus only three months of cash means the investor has weak shock absorption.
- “It has worked for years” is not a downside plan or valuation case.

The fact that they know the company well is relevant, but it does not justify adding leverage. The non-negotiable boundary is: no margin until there is an updated valuation, predefined exit or trim rules, stronger emergency reserves, and a clear answer to “what happens if this drops 40-60% while income is down?”

What would change my judgment: a much stronger balance sheet, stable income, substantial cash reserves, a fresh valuation showing asymmetric upside, and a written risk plan that survives a severe drawdown without forced selling.
