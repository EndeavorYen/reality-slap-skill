My stance: Conditionally agree.

My recommendation: run a two-week discovery track as a separate product/design/research spike, while release ownership stays fully with the reliability owner. The viral feature should not enter the release plan until discovery produces evidence, a bounded scope, and a reliability-safe rollout proposal.

Week 1:
1. Define the hypothesis: what viral behavior is expected, who shares, why they share, and what measurable loop proves it.
2. Interview or test with 5-8 target users.
3. Build only low-cost artifacts: prototype, copy tests, funnel sketches, or fake-door experiment.
4. Identify reliability risks: traffic spikes, abuse, support load, moderation, data exposure, rollback needs.

Week 2:
1. Run the smallest validation experiment that does not touch release-critical systems.
2. Estimate engineering scope and operational cost.
3. Define success metrics: activation lift, invite/share rate, conversion, retention, abuse rate, infra load.
4. Produce a go/no-go brief with three options: drop, continue discovery, or schedule implementation after reliability milestones.

Non-negotiable boundary: discovery can learn, prototype, and quantify; it cannot borrow release engineers, change release gates, or create hidden commitments. If the viral feature is genuinely urgent, leadership must explicitly trade off reliability scope instead of pretending discovery is free.

What would change my judgment: evidence that the viral feature directly improves reliability outcomes or that the release has enough unused capacity to absorb implementation without increasing operational risk.
