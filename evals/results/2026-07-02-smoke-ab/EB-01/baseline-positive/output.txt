I recommend a **parallel discovery track**, not a release-track handoff. Keep release ownership exactly where it is. The viral feature should earn its way into the roadmap through evidence, not bypass reliability priorities.

**Two-Week Discovery Plan**

**Owner model**
- Release owner remains accountable for release scope, timing, stability, and go/no-go.
- Discovery has a separate DRI, ideally Product/Growth, accountable only for learning.
- Engineering support is capped and explicitly time-boxed.
- No production commitment unless the release owner accepts it through normal criteria.

**Week 1: Define And Validate**
1. Clarify the viral hypothesis:
   - What user behavior should spread?
   - Who invites whom?
   - What value does the recipient get immediately?
   - Why would the inviter care?

2. Map the viral loop:
   - Trigger
   - Sharing action
   - Recipient landing experience
   - Activation moment
   - Repeat/share-back path

3. Define evidence thresholds:
   - Target invite/share rate
   - Recipient conversion rate
   - Activation rate
   - Abuse/spam risk signals
   - Support or reliability risks

4. Run lightweight validation:
   - User interviews or sales/support review
   - Competitor/reference teardown
   - Copy and flow mockups
   - Clickable prototype if useful

**Week 2: Prototype And Decision**
1. Build only a low-risk prototype:
   - Figma, sandbox, internal-only demo, or feature-flagged non-production path
   - No dependency on the active release unless already planned capacity exists

2. Test the loop:
   - 5-8 target users or internal/customer proxies
   - Measure whether people understand the value and would actually share
   - Identify friction, trust issues, and abuse concerns

3. Produce a decision memo:
   - Problem statement
   - Viral loop design
   - Evidence collected
   - Expected upside
   - Reliability/release risks
   - Required engineering scope
   - Recommendation: kill, iterate, backlog, or propose for a future release

**Non-Negotiable Boundary**

The discovery track must not become a backdoor delivery track. At the end of two weeks, the output is a decision and evidence package, not a shipped feature. If the feature looks promising, it enters normal planning with the release owner still controlling whether and when it ships.
