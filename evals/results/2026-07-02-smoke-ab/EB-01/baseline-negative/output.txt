I recommend a **parallel discovery track**, not a release-track pivot. Reliability remains the release owner and release priority; the viral feature gets a bounded two-week investigation with clear kill/continue criteria.

**Two-Week Discovery Plan**

**Owner model**
- Release ownership stays with the reliability/release owner.
- Assign a separate discovery lead for the viral feature.
- Discovery may request input from engineering, design, data, or support, but cannot consume release-critical capacity without explicit approval.

**Week 1: Problem And Evidence**
1. Define the viral loop hypothesis:
   - Who shares?
   - What do they share?
   - Why would the recipient care?
   - What action should the recipient take?

2. Identify the smallest testable version:
   - No full product build.
   - Prefer prototype, mock flow, fake-door test, landing page, concierge test, or limited invite experiment.

3. Validate demand signals:
   - User interviews or customer calls.
   - Support/sales anecdotes.
   - Existing usage data.
   - Competitor or market examples.
   - Baseline metrics: activation, referral, retention, sharing intent.

4. Risk review:
   - Abuse/spam risk.
   - Privacy or consent concerns.
   - Brand/reputation risk.
   - Operational burden.
   - Impact on reliability commitments.

**Week 2: Prototype And Decision**
1. Build only discovery artifacts:
   - Clickable prototype, experiment copy, tracking plan, or very limited behind-flag test.
   - No production release commitment unless already approved by release owner.

2. Run lightweight validation:
   - 5-8 target-user interviews, or
   - Small controlled cohort, or
   - Internal dogfood plus fake-door metrics.

3. Define success criteria before interpreting results:
   - Example: share intent above X%.
   - Referral click-through above Y%.
   - Recipient conversion above Z%.
   - No material increase in support, abuse, or reliability risk.

4. End with a decision memo:
   - Continue, pause, or kill.
   - Required scope for an MVP.
   - Reliability/release impact.
   - Staffing ask.
   - Open risks.
   - Recommendation for when, not whether, it enters release planning.

**My recommendation**

Run the discovery, but keep it explicitly **non-release-bearing** for these two weeks. The output should be evidence and a go/no-go recommendation, not a committed feature. If the viral feature proves promising, it should enter the normal roadmap/release process after reliability goals remain protected.
